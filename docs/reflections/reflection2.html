Reflection 2

Midsemester Reflection

Self-assessment and grade argument (Weeks 1-8)

Overview

This reflection asks you to assess your development over the first half of the semester (Weeks 1-8) and argue for a grade based on your work, growth, and engagement.

In keeping with this course's ungrading philosophy, you will not receive numerical scores on individual assignments. Instead, your final grade is the average of two self-assigned grades:

  • This midsemester reflection (covers Weeks 1-8)
  • Final reflection (covers Weeks 9-16)

Your Skills Portfolio serves as the primary evidence for this reflection. Use your completed lab pages to demonstrate growth across the DCDA proficiency strands.

Connection to Skills Portfolio: Your Skills Portfolio (due March 8) is the evidence base for this reflection. Review your work from Labs 1-6 before writing this reflection.

Reflection Template

Use the following template to structure your reflection. Submit as a Word document or PDF.

Required Word Count: 750-1000 words (excluding header information)

Header

Your Name
Date
DCDA 40833 - Midsemester Reflection

Live link to Skills Portfolio: [your GitHub Pages URL]

Overview

Instructions: Write a brief overview (2-3 paragraphs) of your development during Weeks 1-8. What were the highlights? What challenges did you face? How did you grow?

Proficiency Strands

Address each of the following strands with specific examples from your work:

Digital Culture

Instructions: Describe how you've developed digital culture skills during the first half of the semester. Reference specific examples from your portfolio work (HTML/CSS, web design, digital communication, etc.).

Example evidence: Lab 1 (Portfolio Setup), Lab 2 (AI Evaluation), Lab 5 (Vibe Coding)

Data Analytics

Instructions: Describe how you've developed data analytics skills. Reference specific examples from your portfolio work (visualization, critique, Tableau, mapping, data storytelling).

Example evidence: Lab 3 (Visualization Critique), Lab 4 (Tableau), Lab 6 (Python Mapping)

Project Design and Planning

Instructions: Describe how you approached planning and building your portfolio iteratively over multiple weeks. What worked? What was challenging?

Example evidence: Building your portfolio incrementally across Labs 1-6

Self-Direction and Growth

Instructions: Describe how you've taken ownership of your learning. Did you go beyond minimum requirements? How did you respond to feedback? Where did you challenge yourself?

Self-Evaluation

Instructions: Provide an honest evaluation of your work during Weeks 1-8. Consider:

  • Quality and completeness of submitted work
  • Timeliness of submissions
  • Engagement with course material and feedback
  • Areas where you excelled and areas for improvement
  • Any missing or incomplete assignments

Grade Argument

Instructions: Based on the evidence above, argue for the grade you believe you've earned for Weeks 1-8. Be specific and reference your work.

Format: "Based on the above information, I argue that I have earned a(n) [A/B/C/D/F] for the first half of the semester because..."

Remember: Your instructors may adjust your self-assigned grade based on the persuasiveness of your argument and alignment with the evidence you provide.

Guidelines for a Strong Reflection

Be Specific

Don't just say "I learned a lot" — reference specific projects, challenges, and examples from your work.

Be Honest

Acknowledge both strengths and areas for improvement. Growth requires recognizing where you still have room to develop.

Use Evidence

Your Skills Portfolio is your evidence. Reference specific pages, features, and work you've completed.

Connect to Course Goals

Show how your work demonstrates the DCDA proficiency strands: Digital Culture, Data Analytics, Project Design, and Self-Direction.

Make a Case

Your grade argument should be persuasive. Use the evidence you've provided throughout your reflection to support the grade you're proposing.

About Ungrading

This course uses an ungrading model to shift focus from points to learning. Here's how it works:

  • Individual assignments receive qualitative feedback (questions, comments, suggestions) rather than scores
  • Your final course grade is the average of two self-assigned grades: Midsemester (this reflection) and Final
  • You argue for your grade based on work quality, engagement, growth, and timeliness
  • Instructors may adjust your self-assigned grade if the argument doesn't align with the evidence
Key Point: Late work affects your ability to receive timely feedback and demonstrates commitment. Late Semester Projects and Final Reflections are NOT accepted (graduating seniors).
