# Lab 4: Data Visualization in Tableau Public - Evaluation Rubric

## Overview
This rubric uses an ungrading approach to help you understand expectations and assess your own work. It is structured in two parts: a **Completion Checklist** of required deliverables and **Quality Dimensions** where your work can demonstrate different levels of depth and sophistication. This distinction reflects how the assignment actually works — some elements are simply done or not done, while others offer genuine room for excellence.

---

## Part 1: Completion Checklist

The following items are required deliverables. They are evaluated on a complete/incomplete basis — all items must be present for the assignment to be considered submitted. These are not areas where "exceeding expectations" applies; they are simply done or not done.

- [ ] **Tableau visualization created and embedded** — Dataset is from an acceptable source (not the tutorial dataset); visualization is published to Tableau Public and embedded on your portfolio page using Tableau's embed code
- [ ] **Portfolio page linked in navigation** — `lab04.html` exists with proper HTML structure and is accessible from your `index.html` main navigation
- [ ] **Reflection complete** — Written reflection addresses all six prompts (Data Preparation, Design Choices, Expected Patterns, Surprises/Outliers, Questions for Investigation, and Tool Evaluation) and meets the 500-word minimum
- [ ] **Pushed to GitHub and live on GitHub Pages** — Updated portfolio is pushed to the repository and the live site displays correctly
- [ ] **Submitted to D2L** — Both your repository link and live site link are submitted to the D2L dropbox

## Part 2: Quality Dimensions

The following dimensions evaluate the depth and quality of your work. This is where meaningful variation between students occurs, and where you should focus when reflecting on your grade.

---

### 1. Dataset Selection & Domain Knowledge

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Selects a dataset that offers rich opportunities for exploratory analysis with clear metrics and dimensions to investigate
- Demonstrates genuine familiarity with the topic area, drawing on specific prior knowledge when discussing expected patterns
- Dataset choice shows initiative (e.g., self-compiled data, a dataset relevant to their major or interests, or a dataset selected with clear purpose)
- Articulates why this dataset was chosen and what makes it personally or intellectually interesting

#### Meets Expectations
- Selects an appropriate dataset from an acceptable source
- Demonstrates basic familiarity with the topic sufficient to form expectations about patterns
- Dataset provides adequate opportunity for visualization and analysis
- Choice is reasonable and connects to some area of the student's knowledge or interest

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Dataset is poorly suited for visualization or analysis (too small, too uniform, no clear metrics/dimensions)
- Shows no familiarity with the topic; unable to form expectations or identify surprises
- Dataset appears chosen arbitrarily with no connection to student's knowledge or interests
- Uses the tutorial dataset despite instructions to the contrary

---

### 2. Visualization Design

*This evaluates the quality of your visualization and your reasoning about design choices (Prompt 2).*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Visualization effectively communicates patterns in the data through well-chosen chart type(s), metrics, and dimensions
- Design choices are clearly justified — explains *why* specific metrics, dimensions, and chart types were selected, not just *what* they are
- Demonstrates thoughtful use of Tableau's features (appropriate use of color, labels, filters, tooltips, or other elements that enhance readability)
- Visualization could be understood by someone unfamiliar with the dataset
- Shows evidence of iteration or refinement in design

#### Meets Expectations
- Visualization displays data clearly with an appropriate chart type
- Describes the metrics and dimensions selected and provides basic rationale for choices
- Visualization is readable and not misleading
- Tableau features are used adequately (labels present, axes make sense, data is not distorted)

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Visualization is confusing, misleading, or uses an inappropriate chart type for the data
- No explanation of why particular metrics and dimensions were chosen
- Visualization lacks basic readability elements (unlabeled axes, missing legend, unclear what is being shown)
- Little evidence of thought about visual communication

---

### 3. Analytical Depth

*This is the intellectual core of the assignment. It evaluates your pattern recognition (Prompt 3), outlier identification (Prompt 4), and question formation (Prompt 5). These three prompts work together as a coherent analytical arc: predict, observe deviations, ask why.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- **Expected Patterns:** Articulates specific, well-reasoned expectations grounded in domain knowledge; identifies which expectations were confirmed with reference to specific sections of the visualization
- **Surprises/Outliers:** Identifies non-obvious deviations from expectations; goes beyond surface-level observation to describe *why* the deviation is surprising given what was expected
- **Questions for Investigation:** Frames sophisticated, specific questions that could genuinely guide further research — not just "why is this different?" but questions that identify potential causal factors or contextual variables
- The three sections build on each other as a coherent analytical narrative
- Shows genuine intellectual curiosity and comfort with uncertainty

#### Meets Expectations
- **Expected Patterns:** States reasonable expectations based on topic familiarity; identifies what was confirmed with reference to the visualization
- **Surprises/Outliers:** Identifies at least one genuine deviation from expectations; describes the surprise clearly
- **Questions for Investigation:** Poses relevant questions that connect to the identified surprises; questions are specific enough to be answerable
- The three sections are clearly related and follow a logical progression
- Demonstrates honest engagement with the data

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- **Expected Patterns:** Expectations are absent, vague, or clearly reverse-engineered after seeing the data rather than genuine predictions
- **Surprises/Outliers:** Fails to identify meaningful deviations, or claims everything was "as expected" without genuine engagement
- **Questions for Investigation:** Questions are generic, disconnected from the actual data, or absent
- The analytical sections feel disconnected from each other or from the visualization
- Shows little evidence of genuine inquiry or critical thinking

---

### 4. Tool Evaluation

*This evaluates the depth and specificity of your assessment of Tableau Public as a tool (Prompt 6).*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Evaluates Tableau Public with specific, concrete observations grounded in hands-on experience
- Makes specific comparisons to other tools (Excel, Google Sheets, Python libraries, other visualization platforms) with clear articulation of trade-offs
- Identifies limitations encountered during the actual assignment, not just theoretical weaknesses
- Demonstrates nuanced understanding — avoids blanket praise or dismissal; recognizes that strengths and weaknesses depend on context and use case

#### Meets Expectations
- Identifies specific strengths and limitations of Tableau Public based on their experience
- Makes at least one meaningful comparison to another tool
- Limitations discussed are genuine and connected to their use of the tool
- Addresses the prompt's specific questions about what Tableau can and cannot do

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Evaluation is vague or generic (e.g., "Tableau is easy to use" without specifics)
- No comparisons to other tools, or comparisons are superficial
- Limitations are not grounded in actual experience
- Does not address the prompt's specific questions

---

### 5. Writing Quality & Organization

*Word count is checked in the Completion Checklist. This dimension focuses on how well the writing communicates.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Writing is clear, engaging, and well-organized with logical flow between sections
- Arguments and observations are supported with specific evidence and examples from the visualization
- Technical concepts (metrics, dimensions, chart types, data preparation) are explained accurately and accessibly
- Demonstrates careful revision and attention to craft
- Sections build on each other rather than feeling like six disconnected answers

#### Meets Expectations
- Writing is clear and organized; ideas are communicated effectively
- Each prompt is addressed with adequate detail and relevant examples
- Few distracting errors in grammar, mechanics, or formatting
- Shows evidence of proofreading
- Appropriate tone for an academic/professional portfolio

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Writing is unclear, disorganized, or difficult to follow
- Ideas are underdeveloped, repetitive, or lack supporting evidence
- Significant errors in grammar, mechanics, or formatting interfere with communication
- Appears rushed or unrevised
- Content is padded with filler rather than offering substantive reflection

## Self-Assessment Questions

Before submitting, reflect on these questions honestly:

1. **Dataset Fit:** Did I choose a dataset I genuinely know something about? Can I form real expectations and identify real surprises, or am I guessing?

2. **Design Reasoning:** Can I explain *why* I made my visualization choices, or only *what* I chose?

3. **Analytical Honesty:** Did I genuinely predict patterns before looking at the data, or did I reverse-engineer my "expectations" after seeing the results?

4. **Curiosity:** Do my questions for further investigation reflect genuine curiosity, or do they feel like an obligation? Would I actually want to know the answers?

5. **Tool Evaluation Specificity:** Is my Tableau evaluation grounded in my own experience, or could I have written it without ever opening the tool?

6. **Portfolio Quality:** Does this page represent work I would be comfortable showing to a potential employer or graduate program?

- Understand the difference between completing required deliverables and demonstrating quality in your analytical work
- Develop skills in exploratory data analysis and visual communication
- Practice the analytical sequence of prediction, observation, and inquiry
- Self-assess the depth and specificity of your thinking, not just whether you filled in each section

The Completion Checklist ensures all required elements are present. The Quality Dimensions are where you demonstrate growth, intellectual engagement, and the kind of analytical thinking that defines strong DCDA work. When reflecting on your grade, focus on the quality dimensions — that is where the meaningful variation in student work occurs.
