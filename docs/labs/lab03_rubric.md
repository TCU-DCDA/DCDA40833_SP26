# Lab 3: Visualization Critique - Evaluation Rubric

## Overview
This rubric uses an ungrading approach to help you understand expectations and assess your own work. It is structured in two parts: a **Completion Checklist** of required deliverables and **Quality Dimensions** where your work can demonstrate different levels of depth and sophistication. Some elements are simply done or not done, while others offer genuine room for excellence.

---

## Part 1: Completion Checklist

The following items are required deliverables. They are evaluated on a complete/incomplete basis — all items must be present for the assignment to be considered submitted. These are not areas where "exceeding expectations" applies; they are simply done or not done.

- [ ] **Visualization selected from an approved gallery** — Visualization is chosen from Information is Beautiful, Feeling Data Studio, or Data to Art
- [ ] **Critique meets 500-word minimum** — Written critique addresses all required framework components and meets the word count requirement
- [ ] **Visualization embedded with source attribution** — Image of the visualization is embedded on the portfolio page with a working link to the original source
- [ ] **Portfolio page created and linked in navigation** — `lab03.html` exists with proper HTML structure and is accessible from your `index.html` main navigation
- [ ] **Pushed to GitHub and live on GitHub Pages** — Updated portfolio is pushed to the repository and the live site displays correctly
- [ ] **Submitted to D2L** — Both your repository link and live site link are submitted to the D2L dropbox

## Part 2: Quality Dimensions

The following dimensions evaluate the depth and quality of your work. This is where meaningful variation between students occurs, and where you should focus when reflecting on your grade.

---

### 1. Visualization Selection & Source Engagement

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Selects a visualization that offers rich opportunities for critical analysis — not the simplest or most obvious choice from the gallery
- Demonstrates thorough engagement with source material, documentation, or artistic statement
- Shows evidence of exploring multiple visualizations before selecting one
- Articulates clear reasons for the selection that connect to course concepts

#### Meets Expectations
- Selects an appropriate visualization from one of the provided galleries
- Engages with available source material or documentation
- Selection provides adequate opportunity for meaningful critique
- Basic rationale for selection is evident

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Selection lacks substance for critical analysis (too simple, too obvious, nothing to critique)
- Little to no engagement with source material or context
- No clear rationale for selection provided

---

### 2. Application of Analytical Framework

*This evaluates how well you use your chosen framework — Tufte's principles (Option A) or artistic evaluation criteria (Option B) — to analyze specific elements of the visualization. The framework should drive your analysis, not decorate it.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Demonstrates sophisticated understanding of the chosen framework and applies concepts precisely to specific visual elements
- Makes insightful connections between analytical principles and the visualization's design choices
- Goes beyond surface-level observation to uncover deeper implications about how the visualization communicates (or fails to communicate)
- Successfully integrates multiple analytical perspectives (e.g., information resolution *and* additional concerns work together as a coherent argument)

#### Meets Expectations
- Demonstrates solid understanding of the chosen analytical framework
- Applies concepts appropriately to the visualization
- Addresses all required components — information resolution plus two additional concerns (Option A), or data translation plus two critical perspectives (Option B)
- Analysis is clear and grounded in the visualization

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Misunderstands or misapplies analytical concepts
- Analysis is vague or disconnected from the visualization — could describe any visualization, not this specific one
- Missing required components of the framework
- Framework feels tacked on rather than integral to the critique

---

### 3. Critical Thinking & Argumentation

*This is the intellectual core of the assignment. A good critique doesn't just describe — it evaluates, argues, and takes a position on the visualization's effectiveness.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Develops original insights that go beyond obvious observations
- Presents a compelling, well-reasoned argument with strong evidence drawn from specific elements of the visualization
- Considers multiple perspectives — acknowledges what the visualization does well even while critiquing its weaknesses (or vice versa)
- Connects the critique to broader issues in data visualization, communication, or the visualization's subject matter
- Makes sophisticated judgments about effectiveness and integrity

#### Meets Expectations
- Presents clear, logical arguments supported by evidence
- Identifies both strengths and weaknesses in the visualization
- Makes reasonable judgments about effectiveness
- Critique shows independent thinking rather than just summarizing or describing

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Arguments are unclear, unsupported, or contradictory
- Analysis is purely descriptive without critical evaluation
- Fails to identify meaningful strengths or weaknesses — everything is praised or everything is criticized without nuance
- Shows little evidence of independent thinking

---

### 4. Writing Quality & Organization

*Word count is checked in the Completion Checklist. This dimension focuses on how well the writing communicates.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Writing is clear, engaging, and well-organized with logical flow between sections
- Arguments and observations are supported with specific evidence from the visualization
- Technical terminology (Tufte's concepts, design vocabulary) is used accurately and naturally
- Demonstrates careful revision and attention to craft
- Sections build on each other as a coherent critique rather than feeling like disconnected observations

#### Meets Expectations
- Writing is clear and generally well-organized
- Ideas are communicated effectively with appropriate examples
- Few distracting errors in grammar, mechanics, or formatting
- Shows evidence of proofreading
- Appropriate tone for an academic/professional portfolio

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Writing is unclear, disorganized, or difficult to follow
- Ideas are underdeveloped, repetitive, or lack supporting evidence
- Significant errors in grammar, mechanics, or formatting interfere with communication
- Appears rushed or unrevised
- Content is padded with filler rather than offering substantive critique

---

## Self-Assessment Questions

Before submitting, reflect on these questions honestly:

1. **Specificity:** If I removed the name and image of my visualization, could someone reading my critique identify which visualization I'm analyzing? Or is my writing generic enough to apply to anything?

2. **Framework Application:** Did I use Tufte's principles (or artistic evaluation criteria) to analyze *specific visual elements*, or did I just name the concepts and move on?

3. **Balance:** Does my critique acknowledge both strengths and weaknesses, or did I only praise (or only criticize) the visualization?

4. **Source Engagement:** Did I actually read the source material, documentation, or artistic statement — and does my critique reflect that engagement?

5. **Voice:** Does this critique reflect my own thinking, or does it read like a template anyone could have written? Would I be comfortable defending my argument in a class discussion?

6. **Portfolio Quality:** Does this page represent work I would be comfortable showing to a potential employer or graduate program?

---

## Notes on Ungrading Approach

This rubric is designed to help you:
- Understand the difference between completing required deliverables and demonstrating quality in your analytical work
- Develop skills in critical evaluation of data visualization
- Practice applying analytical frameworks to specific artifacts
- Self-assess the depth and specificity of your thinking, not just whether you filled in each section

The Completion Checklist ensures all required elements are present. The Quality Dimensions are where you demonstrate growth, intellectual engagement, and the kind of analytical thinking that defines strong DCDA work. When reflecting on your grade, focus on the quality dimensions — that is where the meaningful variation in student work occurs.
