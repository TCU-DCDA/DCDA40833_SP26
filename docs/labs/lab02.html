AI Experimentation & Evaluation

Choose Your Approach

Select Your Approach

This lab offers two paths to the same destination: developing critical perspective on AI tools. Choose the approach that fits your learning style.

Framework Approach: You'll develop a structured evaluation framework with four dimensions (Capabilities, Appropriate Use, Ethics, Transparency) and apply it systematically to AI tools.

Overview

AI tools are rapidly reshaping how we create, analyze, and communicate. In this lab, you will explore and critically evaluate AI tools relevant to your work as a DCDA student — including text generation, image creation, and code assistance. The goal is not to master any single tool, but to develop a framework for evaluation that you can apply as tools evolve.

Part 1: The Evaluation Framework

When assessing any AI tool, consider these dimensions:

Capabilities

  • What can this tool do well?
  • What are its limitations (what it can't do, or does poorly)?
  • How does output quality vary based on input/prompting?

Appropriate Use

  • What tasks is this tool well-suited for?
  • When might using this tool be inappropriate or counterproductive?
  • How does it fit into a larger workflow (not replacing human judgment)?

Ethical Considerations

  • How was the tool trained? On what data?
  • What biases might be present in its outputs?
  • What are the implications for intellectual property, attribution, and authenticity?
  • What safeguards exist, and what gaps remain?

Transparency & Citation

  • How do you disclose AI use in your work?
  • What does proper attribution look like?
  • How do you verify/fact-check AI-generated content?

Part 2: Hands-On Exploration

Choose at least two AI tools from different categories to explore. At least one should be a tool you haven't used before — this is your chance to discover something new and bring fresh perspective to your evaluation.

Tool Categories

AI tools span many domains. Consider exploring tools from categories like:

  • Text/Chat Assistants — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity, Grok
  • Image Generation — Adobe Firefly, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, Leonardo.AI
  • Writing & Research — Grammarly, NotebookLM, Elicit, Consensus
  • Audio & Voice — Suno, ElevenLabs, Descript, Adobe Podcast
  • Video Production — Runway, Pika
  • Presentation & Design — Canva Magic Studio, Tome, Gamma

All tools listed above have free tiers. You're welcome to explore tools not on this list — the AI landscape changes rapidly, and discovering emerging tools is part of developing your evaluation skills.

Exploration Tasks

For each tool you explore:

  1. Test its capabilities — Give it tasks relevant to your interests or coursework
  2. Push its limits — Find where it struggles or fails
  3. Document your process — Save prompts, outputs, and observations

Part 3: Reflection & Analysis

Write a 500-750 word analysis addressing:

For Each Tool You Explored:

  1. Capabilities: What did the tool do well? Where did it fall short? Include specific examples (screenshots, copied outputs, etc.)
  2. Appropriate Use: Based on your exploration, what would be a good use case for this tool? What would be a poor use case?
  3. Ethical Considerations: What concerns arise from using this tool? How is the developer addressing (or not addressing) these concerns?

Broader Questions:

  1. Personal Use: How do you imagine using AI tools appropriately in your own academic and professional work?
  2. DCDA Context: How might AI tools affect the skills taught in the DCDA program — both positively and negatively? What remains uniquely human in digital culture and data analytics work?
  3. Evolving Landscape: These tools change rapidly. What questions should you ask when evaluating a new AI tool in the future?

Looking Ahead

The evaluation framework you develop here will serve you throughout the semester and beyond. As we integrate AI tools into later labs, you'll apply critical judgment about when and how to use them effectively.

Experimentation Approach: You'll probe, question, and creatively stretch AI tools—focusing on what they reveal about themselves through unconventional use, failures, and surprises.

The Task

Experiment with AI in ways that go beyond typical productivity uses. Your goal is to probe, question, or creatively stretch these tools—not to get a "good" output, but to learn something about how they work, what they assume, and where they break down.

Requirements

  • Use at least two AI tools, at least one you haven't worked with before
  • Try something unconventional—something the tool wasn't designed or marketed for
  • Document your process, including dead ends, failures, and surprises

Possible Directions

These are suggestions, not requirements. You may pursue something else entirely.

Probe for Assumptions

What does the tool assume about you, your task, or "good" output? How do those assumptions surface?

Find the Edges

Where does the tool fail, refuse, or produce nonsense? What triggers those moments?

Compare Responses

Give multiple tools the same unusual prompt. What differs? What does that reveal?

Stretch Creatively

Use a tool for something it clearly wasn't built for. What happens?

Interrogate the Interface

How does the tool's design shape what you can ask and how you ask it?

A Note on Boundaries

This assignment asks you to probe and experiment—but within professional and academic norms. Your experiments and documentation should be appropriate for a classroom setting and a public portfolio. Testing a tool's content moderation by attempting to generate explicit, hateful, or illegal content is not the kind of "edge-finding" this assignment values or your instructors will accept.

Remember: your reflection will be published to your public portfolio. Document experiments you'd be comfortable sharing with future employers or graduate programs.

Your Reflection

Write a 500–750 word analysis that includes:

What You Tried

  • What tools did you explore and why?
  • What experiments did you run? What were you trying to discover?

What Happened

  • What worked, what didn't, what surprised you?
  • Include specific evidence: screenshots, prompts, outputs, failures

What You Learned

  • What did your experiments reveal about how these tools work?
  • What assumptions or biases did you notice?
  • What questions emerged that you couldn't answer?

Looking Forward

  • How might you use (or deliberately avoid) AI tools in your academic and professional work?
  • What questions should you ask when evaluating a new AI tool in the future?

Ethical Considerations

As you experiment, pay attention to:

  • Training & bias: What data shaped this tool? Whose perspectives might be missing or overrepresented?
  • Transparency: Does the tool explain its reasoning? Can you verify its outputs?
  • Attribution: If you use AI-generated content in your work, how should you cite it?

You don't need a separate section on ethics—weave these observations into your reflection where relevant.
