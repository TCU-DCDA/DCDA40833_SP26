# Lab 5: Vibe Coding - Evaluation Rubric

## Overview
This rubric uses an ungrading approach to help you understand expectations and assess your own work. It is structured in two parts: a **Completion Checklist** of required deliverables and **Quality Dimensions** where your work can demonstrate different levels of depth and sophistication.

Students come to this lab with different coding backgrounds — some with HTML/CSS experience, fewer with JavaScript, most with no JS, and all with some Python. The quality dimensions account for this: what matters is not your starting skill level, but the honesty and depth you bring relative to where you are.

---

## Part 1: Completion Checklist

The following items are required deliverables. They are evaluated on a complete/incomplete basis — all items must be present for the assignment to be considered submitted. These are not areas where "exceeding expectations" applies; they are simply done or not done.

- [ ] **At least two experiments completed** — Two or more experiments from the options (Portfolio Enhancement, Python Code Generation, Debug with AI) are attempted and documented
- [ ] **Reflection addresses all nine prompts** — Written reflection covers Technical Understanding (prompts 1-3), Process Evaluation (prompts 4-6), and Ethical & Pedagogical Considerations (prompts 7-9) within the 400-600 word range
- [ ] **Portfolio page with required elements** — `lab05.html` includes a description of what was built, a code snippet commented using the Understanding Spectrum, vibe coding guidelines, and a screenshot or demo
- [ ] **Navigation linked** — Portfolio page is accessible from your `index.html` main navigation
- [ ] **Pushed to GitHub and live on GitHub Pages** — Updated portfolio is pushed to the repository and the live site displays correctly
- [ ] **Submitted to D2L** — Reflection document, commented code, original AI prompts, AI output examples, and links all submitted to the D2L dropbox

## Part 2: Quality Dimensions

The following dimensions evaluate the depth and quality of your work. This is where meaningful variation occurs, and where you should focus when reflecting on your grade.

---

### 1. Experiment Ambition & Effort

*This evaluates whether you challenged yourself relative to your starting skill level. A beginner tackling unfamiliar territory is doing harder work than an experienced coder generating something routine.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Chooses experiments that push beyond current comfort zone — works with unfamiliar languages, tools, or concepts
- Attempts more than the minimum two experiments, or pursues experiments with meaningful scope
- Shows evidence of trying multiple approaches or pushing past initial results, not settling for the first attempt
- Takes on a task where failure was a real possibility, and documents what happened either way

#### Meets Expectations
- Completes two experiments that are appropriate for current skill level
- Experiments are substantive enough to generate meaningful observations about AI-assisted coding
- Shows evidence of following the prescribed process (planning, generating, reviewing, testing, and refining)
- Experiments produce working results

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Experiments are trivially simple regardless of skill level (e.g., asking AI to write a single line of code)
- Only one experiment completed, or second experiment is barely attempted
- No evidence of following the process — no planning, testing, or iteration evident
- Experiments chosen to avoid engagement rather than to learn

---

### 2. Code Understanding & Documentation

*This evaluates whether you understand what the AI generated — not whether you could have written it yourself. Use the Understanding Spectrum from the lab to frame your comments: "I wrote this," "I understand the logic," "I know what it does but not how," and "I don't understand this." Honest acknowledgment of what you don't yet understand is valued over false confidence.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Comments use the Understanding Spectrum to clearly distinguish between parts understood fully, parts understood conceptually, and parts still being learned — this honesty is a strength, not a weakness
- Comments demonstrate genuine understanding of the code's logic, not just restated syntax (e.g., "this filters the array" vs. "this line uses .filter()")
- Identifies specific AI choices they would change and explains why
- Documentation would help another student at a similar level understand the code

#### Meets Expectations
- Code is commented throughout with explanations that go beyond surface description
- Demonstrates understanding of the overall logic and purpose of the code
- Honest about areas of uncertainty — doesn't claim expertise where there is none
- Comments reflect the student's own understanding, not copied AI explanations

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Code has minimal or no comments
- Comments are superficial, copy-pasted from AI explanations, or restate what's obvious from the code
- No acknowledgment of areas not yet understood — either claims full understanding or shows no engagement
- Code was accepted from AI without meaningful review

---

### 3. Critical Reflection

*This evaluates the depth of your thinking about AI as a coding collaborator — not just what happened, but what it means. The reflection must be your own writing, not AI-generated. (Code comments are evaluated separately in Dimension 2.)*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- **Technical Understanding (prompts 1-3):** Reflects on the experience of working with AI-generated code with specificity; modifications are described with clear reasoning about *why* changes were needed, not just what was changed
- **Process Evaluation (prompts 4-6):** Identifies specific moments where AI helped and specific moments where it failed or misled — with concrete examples, not generalizations
- **Ethics & Pedagogy (prompts 7-9):** Personal guidelines are specific and grounded in actual experience from the experiments — not abstract principles that could apply to anyone
- Reflection reads as genuine thinking, not as a performance of what the instructor wants to hear
- Connects observations across all three sections into a coherent perspective on AI-assisted coding

#### Meets Expectations
- Addresses all nine prompts with adequate detail
- Provides specific examples from their experiments rather than speaking in generalities
- Personal guidelines are reasonable and connected to their actual experience
- Reflection is clearly written in the student's own voice
- Shows honest engagement with both the benefits and limitations of AI coding tools

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Prompts are addressed superficially or skipped
- Observations are generic and could apply to anyone without having done the experiments
- Personal guidelines are vague platitudes ("use AI responsibly") with no connection to experience
- Reflection reads as AI-generated or as a formulaic response
- Only discusses benefits or only discusses limitations — no nuance

---

### 4. Process Transparency

*This evaluates how well you documented the collaboration between you and the AI. The prompts you used, the outputs you received, and the changes you made tell the story of your process.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- AI prompts are included and show evidence of thoughtful prompt engineering — specificity, iteration, refinement
- Original AI output is preserved alongside the student's modified version, making the before/after clear
- Documents dead ends, failed attempts, and prompt revisions — not just the successful final product
- A reader could reconstruct the full collaboration from the documentation provided

#### Meets Expectations
- AI prompts and outputs are included in the submission
- Clear distinction between what the AI produced and what the student modified
- Documentation covers the key steps of the process
- Enough evidence to verify the student engaged with the AI meaningfully

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- AI prompts and outputs are missing or incomplete
- No way to distinguish AI contribution from student contribution
- Only the final product is shown with no evidence of process
- Submission lacks the artifacts needed to understand the collaboration (prompts, original outputs, revision history)

---

### 5. Writing Quality & Voice

*Word count is checked in the Completion Checklist. This dimension focuses on whether the writing sounds like you.*

#### Exceeds Expectations
- Writing has a clear, authentic voice — reads as a real person reflecting on a real experience
- Well-organized with logical flow between technical description, process evaluation, and ethical reflection
- Uses specific language and concrete examples rather than vague generalities
- Demonstrates careful revision — ideas are developed, not just listed

#### Meets Expectations
- Writing is clear and organized; ideas are communicated effectively
- Each section is addressed with adequate detail
- Few distracting errors in grammar, mechanics, or formatting
- Tone is appropriate and voice sounds genuine
- Shows evidence of proofreading

#### Does Not Meet Expectations
- Writing is unclear, disorganized, or difficult to follow
- Lacks personal voice — reads as generic or formulaic with no connection to specific experience
- Ideas are underdeveloped or repetitive
- Significant errors interfere with communication
- Content is padded rather than substantive

## Self-Assessment Questions

Before submitting, reflect on these questions honestly:

1. **Know What You Know:** Can I separate what I understand from what I don't? It's fine to say "I understand the logic but not the syntax" — it's not fine to pretend I understand everything when I don't.

2. **The Explanation Test:** If I had to explain my code to a classmate at my same skill level, could I walk them through what it does and why — even if I couldn't write it from scratch?

3. **Modification Honesty:** Did I actually change the AI's output, or did I accept it wholesale? If I changed nothing, was the code genuinely perfect — or did I not look closely enough?

4. **Choosing Your Level:** Did I pick experiments that challenged me at my current skill level, or did I stay in my comfort zone? A beginner tackling something unfamiliar is doing harder work than an experienced coder generating something routine.

5. **Reflection Authenticity:** Is my reflection my own thinking, or did I write what I thought the instructor wanted to hear? Do my "personal guidelines" for AI use reflect how I'll *actually* behave, or are they performative?

6. **Credit Clarity:** If a classmate read my portfolio page, would they understand exactly what the AI contributed, what I contributed, and what I'm still figuring out?

---

## Notes on Ungrading Approach

This rubric is designed to help you:
- Understand the difference between completing required deliverables and demonstrating quality in your engagement with AI tools
- Develop honest self-awareness about what you understand and what you're still learning
- Recognize that challenge relative to your starting point matters more than raw technical output
- Self-assess the authenticity and depth of your reflection, not just whether you filled in each section

The Completion Checklist ensures all required elements are present. The Quality Dimensions are where you demonstrate growth, intellectual honesty, and genuine engagement with AI-assisted development. When reflecting on your grade, focus on the quality dimensions — that is where the meaningful variation in student work occurs.
