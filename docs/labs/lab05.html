Vibe Coding

AI-Assisted Development

Overview

"Vibe coding" refers to AI-assisted code generation where you describe what you want in natural language and the AI produces working code. By now, you've learned HTML/CSS fundamentals (Lab 1), explored AI capabilities critically (Lab 2), and built analysis and visualization skills (Labs 3-4).

In this lab, you'll explore AI as a coding collaborator — learning when it's helpful, when it's problematic, and how to maintain your understanding while leveraging AI assistance.

Key Principle: You must be able to read, explain, and modify any code you use, regardless of who (or what) wrote it.

Before You Apply

Complete every item on this checklist before starting your application. Skipping any of these is the most common reason for rejection.

Pre-Application Checklist

  1. Full legal name on your GitHub profile. This must match your school documents exactly — including middle name or initial if it appears on your student ID. Go to Settings → Public profile to update it.
  2. Billing information filled out. Go to Settings → Billing and licensing and enter your full legal name and address. No payment method is required — GitHub just uses this to verify your identity.
  3. Two-factor authentication (2FA) enabled. Go to Settings → Password and authentication and set up 2FA. This is required.
  4. Add a short bio to your profile. Even one sentence (e.g., "Student at TCU") helps. Go to Settings → Public profile.
  5. Have your school email ready (your @tcu.edu address). This is the fastest verification method.

Application Steps

  1. Log into GitHub and go to Settings → Billing and licensing → GitHub Education.
  2. Click "Start an application" and select the student option.
  3. Enter your school email address (@tcu.edu). If you can verify through email alone, this is the fastest path — approval can come in days rather than weeks.
  4. If email verification isn't enough, you'll be asked to upload proof of enrollment. Acceptable documents include:
    • Student ID (with your name and current dates visible)
    • Enrollment verification letter
    • Class schedule or course enrollment receipt
    • Official transcript with enrollment dates
  5. Use your phone camera to take a photo of the document — do not upload a screenshot, PDF, or pre-existing image file. GitHub's system strongly prefers live camera photos. Make sure the image is well-lit, in focus, and all text is readable.
  6. Submit and wait. Typical turnaround is 7–14 business days from application to full benefit activation.

Common Rejection Reasons (and How to Avoid Them)

  • Name mismatch: Your GitHub profile name doesn't match your documents. Fix this before applying — even small differences (e.g., "Rob" vs. "Robert") can trigger rejection.
  • Blurry or low-quality photo: Use your phone's rear camera in good lighting. All text on the document must be crisp and readable.
  • No 2FA: If two-factor authentication isn't enabled, your application will be automatically rejected.
  • Incomplete billing info: The name and address fields in Billing and licensing must be filled out. This is easy to overlook.
  • Screenshot instead of camera photo: GitHub's verification system expects a live photo capture. Screenshots, scanned PDFs, and gallery uploads have significantly higher rejection rates.
  • Non-English documents without translation: If your document is in another language, photograph it next to a side-by-side English translation.

If You're Rejected

  1. Read the rejection email carefully. It will tell you the specific reason. Don't skip this step.
  2. Fix the issue. Update your profile name, enable 2FA, complete your billing info — whatever the email flagged.
  3. Wait 24–48 hours. GitHub locks reapplication briefly after a denial. Don't try to resubmit immediately.
  4. Take fresh photos. Even if you think your original photos were fine, take new ones with your phone camera. Users report higher approval rates with fresh captures.
  5. Reapply through Settings → Billing and licensing → GitHub Education.
  6. Still rejected? Contact GitHub Support with a clear explanation of what you fixed between attempts.
Apply early. Don't wait until you need Copilot for an assignment. Start the process now so you have time to reapply if necessary.

Part 1: The AI Coding Spectrum

AI coding tools exist on a spectrum. Where you work on this spectrum may shift depending on the task, your familiarity with the language, and what you're trying to learn.

Fundamentals First

You write all the code. AI explains errors and answers questions.

Best for learning new concepts — you stay in control and build understanding from the ground up.

Collaborative

You design the logic. AI helps implement it.

Best when you understand the pattern but need help with unfamiliar syntax or a new language.

Vibe Coding

You describe the outcome. AI generates the code.

Best for rapid prototyping and repetitive tasks — but only if you can read and understand what it produces.

Full Automation

You have minimal involvement. AI is a black box.

Risky — if you can't explain what the code does, you don't really have control over your work.

Remember: You must be able to read, explain, and modify any code you use.

How to Comment: The Understanding Spectrum

When you add comments to AI-generated code, be honest about your level of understanding. Your comments are a record of what you know — not a performance.

  • "I wrote this" — You understand it fully. A brief comment is fine.
  • "I understand the logic" — You get what it does and why, even if you couldn't write it from scratch. Explain in your own words.
  • "I know what it does but not how" — You see the input and output but the mechanism is unclear. Flag it honestly.
  • "I don't understand this" — That's okay to say. Try to figure it out (ask the AI to explain, look it up) and document what you learned.

This honesty feeds directly into your reflection — you'll be able to point to specific moments where you were confident, where you weren't, and what you did about it.

Experiment A: Portfolio Enhancement

Use AI to add a feature to your Skills Portfolio.

Options:

  • CSS animations or transitions (beginner-friendly)
  • Dark mode toggle (beginner-friendly)
  • Image gallery or carousel (intermediate)
  • Responsive navigation menu (hamburger menu on mobile) (intermediate)
  • Accessibility improvements (ARIA labels, keyboard navigation) (advanced)

Process:

  1. Plan first — sketch what you want, identify the components
  2. Prompt the AI — describe the feature clearly
  3. Review the code — read every line and add comments using the understanding spectrum above
  4. Test thoroughly — does it work? Is it accessible? Does it break anything?
  5. Refine — ask AI to modify, or make changes yourself

Experiment B: Python Code Generation

Use Google Colab and AI to generate Python code for a data task. Colab runs in your browser with Python and common libraries (pandas, matplotlib, etc.) already installed — no setup required.

Options:

  • Read a CSV file and create a basic chart (beginner-friendly)
  • Create a specific type of visualization (beginner-friendly)
  • Clean or transform a messy dataset (intermediate)
  • Perform statistical analysis (intermediate)
  • Scrape data from a website (advanced)
  • Process text or perform sentiment analysis (advanced)

Process:

  1. Define the task — be specific about inputs, outputs, and requirements
  2. Generate code — prompt AI with clear instructions
  3. Review line-by-line — add comments using the understanding spectrum above
  4. Test with sample data — does it produce expected results?
  5. Handle edge cases — what breaks it? Ask AI to fix, or fix yourself

Experiment C: Debug with AI

Take broken code and use AI to fix it. Try to identify the bug yourself first, then see how AI approaches it.

Starter Bugs to Try:

  • HTML/CSS: A page where the layout breaks at certain screen sizes, or a nav menu that doesn't link to the right sections (beginner-friendly)
  • JavaScript: A button click handler that doesn't fire, or a loop that produces unexpected output (beginner-friendly)
  • Python: A script that crashes on certain inputs, or a function that returns wrong results for edge cases (intermediate)
  • Your own code: Something from a previous lab or project that you never got working quite right (any level)

What to Compare:

  • Did the AI find the same bug you did, or a different one?
  • Was the AI's fix what you expected, or did it take a different approach?
  • Did the AI introduce any new problems while fixing the original?

Process:

  1. Describe the problem to the AI
  2. Evaluate the fix — does it actually solve the problem?
  3. Understand the root cause — why was the code broken?
  4. Document — comment the fix using the understanding spectrum above; what did you learn about debugging with AI?
Tip: Document your prompts and AI responses as you go — you'll need them for your reflection and submission.

Part 3: Critical Reflection

Write a 400-600 word reflection addressing the following prompts:

Technical Understanding

  1. What code did the AI generate? Describe the feature/function in your own words.
  2. How does it work? Explain the key logic or mechanisms. Where did you land on the understanding spectrum? If there are parts you still don't fully understand after trying, say so — that honesty is more valuable than a confident-sounding explanation you can't back up.
  3. What did you modify? Did you need to fix bugs, adjust styling, or change functionality? Why?

Process Evaluation

  1. When was AI helpful? What tasks did it accelerate or simplify?
  2. When was AI problematic? Where did it produce incorrect, inefficient, or unnecessarily complex code?
  3. What's your role? In an AI-assisted workflow, what remains uniquely human? What can't (or shouldn't) be automated?

Ethical & Pedagogical Considerations

  1. Learning trade-offs: Does vibe coding help or hinder your development as a programmer? Under what conditions?
  2. Attribution: When using AI-generated code, what does proper citation look like? (Consider academic vs. professional contexts)
  3. Your guidelines: What personal rules will you follow for using AI in coding? When will you use it? When won't you?
Important: Do not use AI to write your reflection. This must be your own critical thinking and personal voice.

Self-Assessment

Before submitting, take a moment to reflect on your work honestly:

Questions to Consider

  • Know What You Know: Can I separate what I understand from what I don't? It's fine to say "I understand the logic but not the syntax" — it's not fine to pretend I understand everything when I don't.
  • The Explanation Test: If I had to explain my code to a classmate at my same skill level, could I walk them through what it does and why — even if I couldn't write it from scratch?
  • Modification Honesty: Did I actually change the AI's output, or did I accept it wholesale? If I changed nothing, was the code genuinely perfect — or did I not look closely enough?
  • Choosing Your Level: Did I pick experiments that challenged me at my current skill level, or did I stay in my comfort zone? A beginner tackling something unfamiliar is doing harder work than an experienced coder generating something routine.
  • Reflection Authenticity: Is my reflection my own thinking, or did I write what I thought the instructor wanted to hear? Do my "personal guidelines" for AI use reflect how I'll actually behave, or are they performative?
  • Credit Clarity: If a classmate read my portfolio page, would they understand exactly what the AI contributed, what I contributed, and what I'm still figuring out?

Use these questions to guide final revisions before submission. Honest self-assessment helps you identify areas of strength and opportunities for growth.

The skills you develop here — prompting effectively, evaluating AI output, maintaining code literacy — will serve you throughout the rest of this course and your Semester Project.
