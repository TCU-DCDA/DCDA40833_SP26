Visualization Critique

Evaluating Data Visualization

Overview

Let's apply some of the points from Dr. Crotty's recent lecture (based on Edward Tufte's ideas) to critically evaluate real-world data visualizations. This exercise will sharpen your ability to assess the quality and integrity of visual arguments—a crucial skill for both consuming and creating data-driven content.

Step 1: Select an Infographic

Explore these data visualization galleries and select one infographic that interests you:

Information is Beautiful - Beautiful News | Feeling Data Studio | Data to Art

Once you've selected a visualization, read it closely along with any supporting article or documentation provided.

Step 2: Write Your Critique (500+ words)

Analyze the visualization using Tufte's framework:

Required Analysis

Critique the visualization in terms of what Tufte describes as information "resolution" (169-176). Put another way, how comprehensive and complete is the graphic regarding the information it provides?

If your chosen visualization has an accompanying article or documentation, consider: Is there important information from the source material that is missing in the graphic? If your visualization doesn't have a traditional article (such as artistic data interpretations), consider: What additional context, data, or explanation would enhance understanding? What questions does the visualization leave unanswered?

Additional Concerns (choose at least two)

  • Effects without causes - Does the visualization show outcomes without explaining what produced them?
  • Cherry-picking - Has data been selectively chosen to support a particular narrative?
  • Overreaching - Do the conclusions extend beyond what the data actually supports?
  • Rage to Conclude - Does the visualization rush to conclusions without sufficient evidence?

Your critique should be at least 500 words and demonstrate close reading of the visualization and any available source material, documentation, or artistic statement.

Use this approach if you selected from Feeling Data Studio or Data to Art:

Analyze the artistic data visualization using adapted Tufte principles alongside considerations for creative interpretation:

Required Analysis: Data Translation & Representation

Evaluate how effectively the artwork translates data into visual or sensory form:

  • What data is being represented, and how clear is this translation?
  • What is gained through the artistic interpretation? What might be lost?
  • Does the aesthetic approach enhance or obscure the data's meaning?
  • How does the work balance artistic expression with informational integrity?

Additional Critical Perspectives (choose at least two)

  • Metaphor & Meaning - Are the visual metaphors appropriate for the data? Do they clarify or confuse?
  • Emotional Manipulation vs. Engagement - Does the work use emotion to deepen understanding or to override critical thinking?
  • Accessibility - Can diverse audiences interpret the visualization, or is it overly abstract/exclusive?
  • Context & Completeness - Is sufficient context provided to understand what data is shown and why it matters?

Your critique should be at least 500 words and demonstrate thoughtful analysis of how the artwork functions as both art and information.

Step 3: Add Content and Style

  1. Save the infographic: Download or screenshot the visualization you're critiquing and save it in your portfolio's images/ folder
  2. Replace placeholder text: Add your 500+ word critique, filling in each section with your analysis
  3. Update the image path: Make sure the src attribute points to your saved image
  4. Add the source link: Include the URL to the original visualization
  5. Style consistently: Ensure your page uses the same CSS as the rest of your portfolio
