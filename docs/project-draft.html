Overview

Please submit your Rough Draft and your Draft Reflection Letter to the D2L dropbox by the due date below. Your rough draft should include both your written content and a working draft of your deliverable.

Due: Tuesday, April 21, 2026 by class time

Submit your written draft, deliverable, and Draft Reflection Letter to the D2L dropbox.

Please refer to the Recommended Outline below for insights into how to structure your written content.

Written Content

Your rough draft should include a working version of the written sections of your project. These sections form the narrative backbone of your project and should be developed enough for your peers to offer substantive feedback. See the Recommended Outline below for the suggested structure.

The written content does not need to be polished or complete, but it should be developed enough to convey the scope and direction of your project.

Deliverable

In addition to the written content, you should include a working draft of your project deliverable. Depending on the project track you chose, this might include:

Data Analysis (DA) Track

A working draft of your data analysis, which may include code, visualizations (graphs, charts, maps, word clouds, etc.), and/or a preliminary version of your web-based presentation of results.

Digital Culture (DC) Track

A working draft of your digital artifact, which may include a website, interactive media, multimedia content, or another multimodal authoring project.

The deliverable does not need to be finished, but it should be functional enough that your peers and instructors can see where it's headed and provide meaningful feedback.

Draft Reflection Letter

Before you submit your rough draft, compose a Draft Reflection Letter (as a Word doc, a minimum of 500 words) to the classmate(s) you'll work with during the Peer Review workshop. In your letter, please dedicate a short paragraph to each of the following bullet points as a way of giving your classmates insight into 1) what your intentions are for the draft and deliverable, 2) what you think you've accomplished so far, and 3) what you still think you need to do to make your project even better.

  • Summarize your project in one or two sentences.
  • What surprised you while crafting the written content and building the deliverable?
  • What aspect(s) of assembling the written draft and/or the deliverable presented the greatest challenge for you?
  • What successes have resulted from the project so far — in both the writing and the deliverable?
  • What is your vision for the final version of your written content and deliverable? What still needs work?
  • Enumerate three (3) substantive questions about your project that you'd like your peers to address in their written replies. What do you need help with — technically, conceptually, or in terms of the deliverable? What are you still trying to sort out? (Ask real questions! The more thoughtful the questions, the better your feedback will be!)

Your letter will serve as a cover memo for this draft. Failure to submit a Draft Reflection Letter will affect your semester grade.

Recommended Outline for Written Content

Below, you'll find an outline that should work for most projects this semester. If you have any questions about what each section is calling for — or if you feel your project requires a different organization — please let us know!

Introduction (index.html)

This section will serve as an abstract of the project. You can think of it as an introduction to the site that provides an overview of the content that follows.

An abstract is a self-contained, short, and powerful statement that describes a larger work. An abstract of a social science or scientific work may contain the scope, purpose, results, and contents of the work. An abstract of a humanities work may contain the thesis, background, and conclusion of the larger work. An abstract is not a review, nor does it evaluate the work being abstracted. While it contains key words found in the larger work, the abstract is an original document rather than an excerpted passage. (UNC Writing Center)

Literature Review (literature.html)

In this section, you should expand, refine, and/or clarify the Literature Review you wrote for your Project Proposal.

Note: This section is not a general background that details your personal interest in the topic. It's a literal overview of the literature related to your topic. By "literature," we mean studies and other sources that have preceded and are relevant to your topic.

What is a Literature Review?

A Literature Review surveys source materials that are relevant to a person's research thesis/problem and/or a particular issue or theory. It also provides a critical analysis that summarizes and synthesizes the source materials while also demonstrating how a person's research pertains to or fits within the larger discipline of study.

Good literature reviews help answer two questions:

  • What do we know about this particular issue, theory, or subject?
  • What do we not know about this particular issue, theory, or subject?

They also evaluate context, explain relationships between works, identify gaps, define new ways to interpret research, address conflicts in contradictory research, and identify the need for additional research.

Research Methodology (method.html)

In this section, you should expand, refine, and/or clarify the Research Methodology section you wrote for your Project Proposal. Walk your reader through the specific techniques and methods you used for your project, connecting your methodology directly to your Research Question.

Note: Be methodical in your presentation. Provide very specific descriptions of each step you took in data collection, data management, and data analysis.

This section should include, but is not limited to:

  • Methods of data collection (from a shared data resource, on your own)
  • Methods of analysis (sources of code, tools used, techniques used)
  • Choices you made to refine or limit your project to make it more manageable

You should further explain whether your methods are quantitative or qualitative (or both), and how your choices were necessary to answer your Research Question. If you borrowed code or methods from particular online sources, credit them here (and again in the Works Cited section).

Analysis & Findings (findings.html)

This is where the rubber meets the road. In this section, walk the reader through what you've learned and discovered through your analysis. If you've produced any visualizations of your results or findings (word clouds, graphs, maps, etc.), you should include and discuss them here.

Conclusion (conclusion.html)

In this final section, explain how well you feel the project answers your Research Question and what your project contributes to the larger conversation related to your topic. Also discuss, when possible, the additional questions your project has raised — what new questions can we ask now that your Research Question has been addressed?

Works Cited

A list of all the sources you used for the project in APA or MLA format. Please include direct links to online sources.
