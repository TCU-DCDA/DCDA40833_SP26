Introduction (index.html)

This section will serve as an&nbsp;abstract of the project. You can think of it as an introduction to the site that provides an overview of the content that follows. More specifically:

An abstract is a self-contained, short, and powerful statement that describes a larger work… An abstract of a social science or scientific work may contain the scope, purpose, results, and contents of the work. An abstract of a humanities work may contain the thesis, background, and conclusion of the larger work. An abstract is not a review, nor does it evaluate the work being abstracted. While it contains key words found in the larger work, the abstract is an original document rather than an excerpted passage. (https://writingcenter.unc.edu/tips-and-tools/abstracts/)




Literature Review (literature.html)

In this section, you should expand, refine, and/or clarify the Literature Review you wrote for your Project Proposal.

Note: This section is&nbsp;NOT&nbsp;a general background that details your personal interest in the topic. It’s a literal&nbsp;overview&nbsp;of the&nbsp;literature related to your topic. By “literature,” we mean studies and other sources that have preceded and are relevant to your topic.

The following is adapted from&nbsp;http://guides.lib.ua.edu/c.php?g=39963&amp;p=253697:

Definition:

A Literature Review surveys … source materials that are relevant to a person's research thesis/problem and/or a particular issue or theory. It also provides a critical analysis that&nbsp;summarizes&nbsp;and&nbsp;synthesizes&nbsp;the source materials while&nbsp;also&nbsp;demonstrating how a person's&nbsp;research&nbsp;pertains&nbsp;to or fits within the larger discipline of study.

Purpose:

Literature Reviews vary from discipline to discipline as well as across assignments, but generally a good literature review is designed to help you answer 2 questions:

What do we know about this particular issue, theory or subject?

What do we&nbsp;not&nbsp;know about this particular issue, theory or subject?

Good literature reviews also:

Evaluate the context of scholarly material for its contribution to the understanding of the research thesis being studied.

Explain the relationships between each of the works under deliberation.

Identify gaps in previous research.

Define new ways to interpret research within a discipline.

Address conflicts found in contradictory&nbsp;research&nbsp;previously conducted.

Identify the need for additional research.




Research Methodology (method.html)

In this section, you should expand, refine, and/or clarify the Research Methodology section you wrote for your Project Proposal.

More specifically, you will walk your reader through the specific techniques and methods you used for your project. You should connect your description of your methodology directly to your Research Question.

Note: You should be&nbsp;methodical&nbsp;in your presentation of the information in this section. Specifically, you should provide&nbsp;very specific descriptions of each step you took in data collection, data management, and data analysis.

This section should include, but is not limited to:

Methods of data collection (from a shared data resource, on your own)

Methods of analysis (sources of code, tools used, techniques used).

Choices you made to refine or limit your project to make it more manageable

You should further explain whether your methods are quantitative or qualitative (or both), and how your choices were necessary to answer your Research Question.

If you borrowed codes or methods from particular online sources, you should credit them here (and again in the Works Cited section).




Analysis &amp; Findings (findings.html)

This is where the rubber meets the road, if you will. In this section, you will walk the reader through what you’ve learned and discovered through your analysis. If you’ve produced any visualizations of your results or findings (word clouds, graphs, maps, etc), you should include and discuss them here.




Conclusion (conclusion.html)

In this final section, you will provide an explanation as how well you feel the projects answers your Research Question and what your project contributes to the larger conversation related to your topic. It also discusses, when possible, the additional questions your project has raised (as in, what new questions can we ask now that your Research Question has been addressed?).
