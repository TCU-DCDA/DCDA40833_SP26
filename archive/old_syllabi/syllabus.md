# [Course Description]{.smallcaps}

## Catalog Description

This course is designed as a capstone experience for students in the
Digital Culture & Data Analytics (DCDA) Minor. Hence, the course is
designed to give students the

opportunity to develop a semester long project that will allow them to
practice, reflect upon, and publicly showcase their work as students in
the DCDA program.

## Prerequisites & Concurrent Enrollment

MATH 10043 or INSC 20153 and GEOG 30323, WRIT 20833, or COSC 10603

# [Teaching Philosophy & Methodology]{.smallcaps}

We usually begin our course introduction by saying, *"If class time is
spent with only the instructors talking, this course is going to suck
for all of us."* So, to that end, our main ambition is for each student
to have a growing confidence in their ability to engage in the course
material. Our role is to provide as much useful context as possible to
get the discussion and your Semester Projects *started*. Whatever we
bring to the conversation, **we want the students to push each other and
themselves** to take the conversation and their projects *further*.
We'll all learn more -- and you'll have a lot more fun in the process
\-- if we're exploring the course materials, tools, and issues together.

Early in the semester, class time will be spent in discussion,
demonstrations, and lab assignments intended to help you refine the
skills needed for your Semester Project. As the semester moves on, more
and more class time will be spent on tasks directly related to your
Semester Projects.

# [Course Requirements]{.smallcaps}

## Assignments

The first half of the course will allow you to update your digital
skills toolbox and/or give you practice using tools you might
incorporate into your Semester Project. This part of the course requires
weekly assignments (or **labs**) that will culminate in a **DCDA Skill
Portfolio & Midsemester Reflection.**

The second half of the course will allow you to focus on developing
(solo or in a group) a **Semester Project** that takes a "deeper dive"
into a topic of your choosing broadly connected to digital culture
and/or data analytics. To prepare for the final draft, we'll ask roughly
midsemester that you submit a **Project Proposal (w/ Bibliography).**
Closer to the end of the semester, we'll also ask that you submit at
least 1 if not 2 **Rough Drafts**.

While we want you to be able to use your **Skill Portfolio** and your
**Semester Project** for your next steps, post-graduation (as part of
job or grad school applications, for example), we encourage you to
explore topics that venture firmly outside narrow professional
interests. Our reasoning: you'll spend the better part of the rest of
your life as a "professional" of some sort. Consider approaching this
class as one of your "last" opportunities, at least as an undergraduate
student, to explore a topic of personal interest, you know, for its own
sake.

## Grading Philosophy & Policy

**Ungrading**

**(Adapted with much gratitude from Jesse Stommel's syllabus available
here: <https://dgst101.com/dgst101-fall-2020-syllabus-302b22518009)>**

This course will focus on qualitative as opposed to quantitative
assessment, something we'll discuss during the class. While you will get
a final grade at the end of the term, we will not be grading individual
assignments, but rather asking questions and making comments that engage
your work rather than simply evaluating it numerically. You will

also be reflecting carefully on your own work and the work of your
peers. The intention here is to help you focus on working in a more
organic way, as opposed to working as you think you're expected to. If
this process causes more anxiety than it alleviates, see us at any point
to confer about your progress in the course to date. If you are worried
about your grade, your best strategy should be to attend class, join the
discussions, do the readings, and complete all the assignments in a
timely manner. All this said: **you should consider this course a
"busy-work-free zone." If an assignment does not feel productive, talk
to us about ways we can modify, remix, or repurpose the instructions.**

Your grade for the semester will the average of 2 equally weighted
grades.

The **first grade** will be based on the case you build for the grade
you believe you deserve for the work you've done in the first half of
the semester in the context of the feedback given you over the first 8
weeks. You'll make this argument in your **Midsemester Reflection**.
***Please grade yourself using the +/- system.***

The **second grade** will be based on the case you build for the grade
you believe you deserve for the work you've done in the second half of
the semester in the context of the feedback given over the second 8
weeks. You'll make this argument in you **Final Course Reflection.** Dr.
Crotty and Dr. Rode will meet to discuss your arguments for your grades
and reserve the right to adjust your grades based on the persuasiveness
of your case. Your actual grades may be higher or lower than the grade
you give yourself. ***Please grade yourself using the +/- system.***

### Late Work

Which takes us to **Late Work**: Our schedule is tight, and you have
some meaningful and important work to do. In the context of our
Ungrading policy, we see assignment deadlines as milestones to keep you
on track for the Skill Portfolio and Semester Project.

"Failure" to submit your work in a timely fashion will affect our
ability to provide timely and needed feedback on your work and could
affect the persuasiveness of the arguments you make for your midterm and
semester grades.

**One exception: Given that most students in the class are graduating
seniors, late Semester Projects and Final Reflections will not be
accepted. Our turnaround to report grades will be especially tight, and
we won't have the time to track down late submissions of these final
assignments.**

In the event of an emergency or serious illness, every reasonable effort
should be made to let us know that you're unable to meet a stated
deadline.

### Participation, Engagement & Attendance

For us, our course material is best learned through conversation and
hands-on practice, not lecture. The experience of any course comes not
from the purported "brilliance" of the professors but from the
engagement and trust of the broader classroom community. Trust develops
over time spent in each other's company.

In keeping with our Ungrading policy, we will not "grade you" on
attendance and have no official attendance policy. But please
understand, it is our responsibility to keep track of your participation
in the various elements of the course 1) to make sure everyone is making
steady progress and 2) for reference should you have a grade appeal at
the end of the term.

Please also understand that infrequent attendance could also affect the
persuasiveness of the arguments you make for your midterm and semester
grades.

Beyond that, we refer you to The *[[University Attendance
Policy]{.underline}](https://campuslife.tcu.edu/students/university-attendance-policy/)*
that regular and punctual class attendance is essential and that no
assigned work is excused because of absence, no matter what the cause.

## Participation

Given the hands-on and potentially collaborative nature of the work
we'll do together this semester, active and earnest participation is one
of our baseline expectations.

You'll learn a lot from the course's instructors. You'll learn even more
from each other. The quality and consistency of your participation and
engagement with the course community will be something you'll want to
discuss in your 2 course reflections as you build a case for your
semester grade.

### Technology Policies

**Artificial Intelligence (AI) Ethical Considerations and Consequences
for Misuse**

The inappropriate or unauthorized use of AI-generated content may be
academic misconduct and/or a violation of discipline-specific
professional ethics. Such misuse of AI or other assignment-help tools
will be handled according to TCU's Academic Conduct Policy or other
relevant policies and may result in sanctions, including failing the
course, program dismissal, suspension, or expulsion.

[Course Policy Regarding AI:]{.underline}

Artificial intelligence may be used for any assignment with appropriate
citation. Examples of such citations can be found online, for example at
[[APA]{.underline}](https://apastyle.apa.org/blog/how-to-cite-chatgpt).
You are responsible for fact-checking statements composed by AI. The use
of AI should not replace your critical thinking, judgment, and topic
expertise.
